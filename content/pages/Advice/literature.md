Just talking about "Related Work" (for a paper) is a little weird - while that's the focus here, it really needs to be in the context of generally understanding the literature "around" your research. Also, many papers have things that are like related work, but different (Background, Literature Review, Survey, etc.).

## Related Work in a Paper

A related work section in a research paper should be that: it should explain the **relation** between other work and yours. 

There are many different kinds of relationships:

1. Background (for you): things you needed to know in order to do the work.
2. Background (for the reader): things the reader needs to know in order to read your paper.
3. Inspirations: things that inspired/informed your work.
4. Contrasts: things that you want to **differentiate** your work from so that you can claim a contribution. This includes explaining why a piece of work is *irrelevant*. 
5. General Survey: things described for no clear reason related to the research in question.

Some commentary:

+ Help the reader by letting them know **why** related work is related (e.g., it should be clear which of the above you are doing)
+ The focus of most related work sections in papers is #4, which should be explicit. Make the differences and similarities clear. 
+ In most research papers, #5 can be avoided: it is a distraction. 
+ Don't overlook #1 and #2 - it is important, but it is different from #4. 
+ #3 often turns into #1, #2 or #4. If you got an idea from somewhere, the reader might need to understand it, you needed to figure out how to build off of it, and you need to explain why it didn't solve your problem.
